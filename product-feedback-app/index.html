Product Feedback App

This was the last project I worked on during my internship at Colgate-Palmolive.

This app does two things: remind testers to submit a response, and provide a mobile interface to curate their scheduled feedback.

Role
UX Design, UI Design

  • Background

    Right around Christmas 2016, my manager sat me down to brief me regarding the following week as he was going to be on leave. He mentioned about a new mobile app that will go into design and development at the end of January and asked if I could come up with the design for a few components. Seeing a chance for me to work on something no one has touched yet, I quickly accepted.

    He told me that basically, people sign up to be testers of certain consumer products before and during release, and part of the terms was to provide their honest feedback periodically. However, this was still being done physically through survey forms that then have to be collected and entered into the system. This was obviously very inefficient and out-dated, so business wanted a mobile application.

    He then provided me with a few of the survey forms currently being used and told me I had all the creative freedom.

  • Approach

    I decided to tackle the most important part of this app first: the survey. After spending about a day looking at the survey forms and noted down the type of questions on each survey, their posible answers, and format of questioning, I noticed that they generally fall into one of four types: binary questions, scale-type questions, multiple-choice questions, and open ended questions. Most questions also accepted 'Not Applicable' as a response.

    Binary questions are simple questions with two answers at opposite extremes. Scale-type questions are like binary questions, with more intermediate options (normally around 7-10 in total) between the two extremes. Multiple-choice questions are questions with multiple options for responses that do not lie on a spectrum. Finally, open-ended questions provided a space for the tester to freely write their thoughts. Scale-type questions appear substantially more frequently than the others.

    As there were almost no contraints and I clearly understood the problem at hand, so I decided to start my research by looking at similar mobile survey apps such as SurveyMonkey, OpinionMeter, LoopSurvey, and even Google Forms. I took note of elements from each app that I was convinced will serve the app well.

  • Question & Response Design

    Binary questions

    For binary questions, a two simple buttons were used to display the options side by side. At most one of these can be selected at any one time.

  • Scale-type questions

    It was challenging coming up with a design for these type of questions mainly because for each quetion, there are about 7-10 options, and the labels for their options can be rather long. In the end, I decided to go with a sliding Likert scale and a label under it.

  • Multiple-choice questions

    There are two types of multiple-choice questions, one that allows multiple-selection and one that doesn't. I decided not to reinvent the wheel and stick to the familiar standard of radio buttons and checkboxes.

  • Open-ended questions

    There wasn't much to innovate regarding the open-ended questions. A simple text input area with a standard cursor to indicate it's function suffices.

  • For the N/A case

    The 'Not Applicable' response is slightly different than all the other responses. It doesn't quite fit on a scale, and I didn't think it should warrant it's own radio button.

    After a night of pondering, I came to thinking that gestures might be the answer (no pun intended). After all, this was meant to be a smart phone app. So I decided swiping from right to left will grey out the question, and a label was added on the bottom to clarify its state. A grey 'N/A?' button was added to the bottom right so users can tap to grey out the question too.

    Hopefully, by putting the N/A option outside the flow of the regular options, we can reduce the frequency of testers satisficing,, thus providing better feedback. If a tester needs to answer 'Not Applicable' to many questions on a survey, it's clear that they are either not suited for the test product, or more appropriate questions should be asked.

  • Obligatory questions

    Following convention, an asterisk was added to the top right of the question tile if a response was required. Upon submission, unanswered obligatory questions will shake and be bordered red.

  • Designing other elements

    Progress indicator

    Surveys are often time-consuming and tedious, adding a progress indicator is an easy way to motivate a user to carry on by showing them how close they are to finishing.

    Time estimation

    Estimated time to complete a survey is provided prior to beginning a survey. This increases the chances that a tester will complete the survey in one sitting, meaning that they would be in the same frame of mind when answering all the questions. This yields more meaningful and consistent feedback data.

    Late response notification

    As feedback are requested on a schedule, users should be notified when they should take it or when its past due. To do this, the survey item's border changes from green (good time to complete), to orange (recently due), and lastly to red (very late).

  • What do you think?

    This was a fun little way to end my internship at Colgate, mainly because business and technology restrictions were not defined yet and I so I had all the freedom to come up with relevant designs. This ended up being a challenge for me to design the ideal survey app for a packaged product company like Colgate-Palmolive.
